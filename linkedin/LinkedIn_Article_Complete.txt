When I published Part I, I argued that space-based AI data centers can stay cool. The replies pushed back: cooling is the easy part. They were right, so I rebuilt the whole thing as a complete systems study, from primary sources, and corrected my own mistakes.

Part II covers every subsystem, not just thermal: power, attitude control and optical pointing, the communications link budget, radiation, reliability, debris, formation flying, propulsion and disposal, compute throughput, and economics, all closed into one self-consistent model.

The honest verdict:

🛰️ Cooling is solved and flight-proven (Starcloud flew an H100 in orbit, Nov 2025). But it does not scale: radiator area grows linearly (~1,500 m² per MW), and above ~400 W per chip the passive heat path alone blows the entire 125 C budget, so big GPUs need liquid cooling at the die.

📡 The bus cannot point the laser. A star tracker gives ~10 microradians; a laser link needs under 1. A fine-steering mirror is mandatory, not optional.

☄️ Debris is the binding risk: ~17% central chance of a catastrophic hit per 81-satellite cluster over 5 years, and a tight formation turns one hit into a cascade.

🌍 It will not deorbit itself: ~22-year natural decay at 650 km fails the FCC 5-year rule. Active de-orbit is mandatory.

💰 Launch cost is no longer decisive: below ~$600/kg, hardware dominates.

Everything is open and reproducible: a 13-subsystem Python model with 23 validation checks. Full article and code below. Corrections welcome.

SPACE-BASED AI DATA CENTERS, PART II
A Complete Systems Study: Survivability, Subsystems, and the Path to a Demonstrator

By Samarjith Biswas, PhD
A continuation of the November 2025 thermal study. Reference architecture: Google Project Suncatcher (arXiv:2511.19468). Every number is sourced; every model is open and reproducible.


WHY A PART II

Part I asked one narrow question: can passive radiation keep a TPU-class chip within its temperature limit in low Earth orbit? Yes. But readers correctly pointed out that a chip is not a spacecraft. A real orbital data center is a power system, an attitude-control and pointing system, a laser-communications mesh, a radiation-hardened compute fabric, a debris-survival problem, a propulsion and disposal problem, and an economics problem, all at once. This Part II models all of them, corrects the places where Part I was wrong, and closes the subsystems into one self-consistent design.


WHAT I CORRECTED

- Disposal: the rule is now 5 years, not 25 (FCC, 2022, effective 2024). At 650 km natural decay is ~22 years, so it fails. Active de-orbit is mandatory.
- Chip power: Google publishes no TDP for the TPU v6e; the honest estimate is ~150 to 200 W, not the 300 W I assumed.
- Atmospheric density at 650 km: 1.6 x 10^-13 kg/m³ (moderate solar), swinging ~100x over the solar cycle.
- Debris flux above 1 cm: 1 x 10^-5 to 1 x 10^-4 per m² per year.
- Radiator accounting: double-sided is the physical case.


THE VERIFIED REFERENCE SYSTEM

Dawn-dusk sun-synchronous orbit, 650 km. An 81-satellite cluster on a 1 km-radius lattice with 100 to 200 m spacing. Optical links at 800 Gbps one-way (1.6 Tbps two-way), 1.55 micron. TPU v6e: 918 TFLOPS BF16, 32 GB HBM; radiation-tested in a 67 MeV proton beam; 5-year requirement 750 rad(Si); HBM is the limiter at ~2 krad. Flying hardware for scaling: Starcloud's orbital H100 (700 W, Nov 2025); Blackwell B200/GB200 (1000 to 1200 W) next.


1. THERMAL: SOLVED, BUT IT DOES NOT SCALE

Radiated power per unit area is fixed by the fourth-power law, so radiator area is linear in heat load:
   A_rad = Q / (ε · σ · T⁴ · n_sides · (1 − f_parasitic))
A 1 MW node needs ~1,500 m² at 20 C (consistent with the ~1,200 m²/MW industry rule). There is also a sharper wall at the chip: with a passive chain of thermal resistance R_th,
   ΔT_interface = P · R_th
At R_th = 0.30 K/W this is 90 C at 300 W but 210 C at 700 W and 360 C at 1200 W, exceeding the entire 125 C budget. Above ~400 W, chip-level liquid or loop-heat-pipe cooling is mandatory. This is why Starcloud and Axiom use pumped loops, not the passive vapor chamber I assumed.
(Figure 2: radiator area vs power; interface saturation.)


2. POWER: THE EPS AND THE DAWN-DUSK ADVANTAGE

Triple-junction GaInP/GaAs/Ge cells deliver ~29.6% at beginning of life and ~27.5% after a 5-year LEO fluence, derated ~0.23 %rel per C for hot operation. Array power at the load is
   P = S0 · A · η(T, life) · packing · path_efficiency,  S0 = 1361 W/m²
The dawn-dusk orbit is the key lever: it rides the terminator and stays 95 to 99% sunlit year-round, so the battery only covers a short seasonal eclipse rather than ~16 deep cycles a day. That removes the dominant battery mass and cycle-life constraint of normal LEO.
A caution that falls straight out of the budget: for a small (4-chip) satellite, non-compute overhead (avionics, ADCS, comms, thermal, contingency) dominates, giving an electrical PUE near 1.6. The efficiency case improves only at scale, with many chips per bus.
(Figure P: array sizing and degradation.)


3. ATTITUDE AND OPTICAL POINTING: THE BUS CANNOT POINT THE LASER

This is the subsystem Part I omitted entirely, and it is decisive. A laser inter-satellite link needs pointing accuracy under 1 microradian. A good star tracker gives ~2 arcseconds, which is ~10 microradians, and reaction-wheel microvibration adds more. The bus is one to two orders of magnitude too coarse. The resolution is a coarse-gimbal plus a fine-steering mirror that closes its own sub-microradian loop on the beacon, with wheel isolation.
Beam divergence sets the difficulty: Θ ≈ 2.44 λ/D. Encouragingly, the 100 to 200 m neighbor links are trivial (a 20 microradian beam is millimetres wide at 200 m). The hard pointing is the ground link and any long inter-satellite hops. There is also a real attitude conflict: radiators want to be edge-on to the sun, arrays want to face it, and terminals must track neighbors and ground. The standard resolution is articulation: gimballed arrays and gimballed optical terminals, so one rigid body does not have to satisfy all three.
(Figure C: pointing-loss sensitivity.)


4. COMMUNICATIONS: THE LINK BUDGET AND WHY THE FORMATION IS TIGHT

The optical link budget is
   P_rx = P_tx + G_tx + G_rx − L_fs − L_point − L_atm − L_optics
with telescope gain G = (π·D/λ)² and free-space loss L_fs = (4π·R/λ)². The range term is everything. At λ = 1.55 micron with a 0.1 m aperture, free-space loss is ~184 dB at 200 m but ~258 dB at 1000 km, a 74 dB gap. That is the entire reason Suncatcher flies its satellites ~150 m apart: it buys back enormous link margin and makes 800 Gbps on one transceiver pair realistic. NASA's TBIRD has shown 200 Gbps space-to-ground; cloud cover, not power, is the ground-link bottleneck, solved by geographically diverse optical ground stations. On latency, light in vacuum beats fiber (n = 1.4675), so a LEO optical mesh beats terrestrial fiber for paths beyond ~3,000 km.
(Figure C: free-space loss vs range.)


5. RADIATION AND RELIABILITY OF THE COMPUTE

Total ionizing dose behind shielding is
   D(x) = D0 · e^(−x/λ) + D_floor
At 10 mm of aluminium the 5-year dose is ~1.06 krad(Si), under the ~2 krad HBM tolerance with ~1.9x margin. The real cost is single-event upsets. Because AI inference tolerates bit-flips far better than traditional HPC, the right design is ECC plus checkpointing (~86% usable compute), not full triple-modular redundancy (which halves throughput). Carry this ~10 to 20% throughput tax explicitly in any efficiency claim.
(Figure 4: dose vs shielding; reliability vs throughput.)


6. RELIABILITY AND AVAILABILITY: NO ON-ORBIT REPAIR

A failed chip in orbit cannot be swapped, so reliability is bought up front. Exponential reliability R(t) = exp(−t/MTBF) is unforgiving: a bus with MTBF = 50,000 h has only R(5 yr) ≈ 0.42, so half the fleet would be dead without redundancy. Availability is therefore a constellation property, maintained by over-provisioning, graceful degradation, and continuous replenishment (a 5-year design life implies ~20% per year replacement to sustain). Within a satellite, k-of-n redundancy on the chips sets the spares; across the fleet, the expected available capacity is min(1, n_deployed · R / n_required). This is a launch-cadence and sparing problem that Part I never addressed.
(Figure R: R(t) and over-provisioning.)


7. ORBITAL DEBRIS AND THE IN-CLUSTER CASCADE

The Poisson probability of at least one catastrophic impact in the cluster is
   P = 1 − exp(−Φ · A · N · t)
With Φ = 3 x 10^-5 per m² per year (ORDEM/MASTER central), A = 15 m², N = 81, t = 5 yr, P = 16.7% (6 to 46% across the model band). The tight formation then couples failures: a fragmentation sends N_f lethal fragments over a sphere 4π·d², so a neighbor at range d is struck with
   P_hit = 1 − exp(−N_f · r_t² / (4·d²))
which is ~8.5% per neighbor at 150 m. Widening to 1 km cuts that below 1% but degrades the optical-link budget. That is the formation paradox, and it is the binding, unsolved constraint.
(Figure 3: cluster risk and cascade coupling.)


8. FORMATION FLYING AND RELATIVE DYNAMICS

Holding 150 m is a control-and-sensing problem, not a fuel problem. The relative motion follows the Clohessy-Wiltshire equations about the reference orbit (radial and cross-track modes oscillate at the mean motion n = √(μ/a³)); the danger is the secular along-track drift driven by differential drag between satellites of slightly different ballistic coefficient, which grows quadratically in time. With a 150 m spacing budget and density that swings ~100x over the solar cycle, the margin is thin, and the same proximity that enables the links makes a debris-avoidance maneuver a hazard to neighbors.


9. END OF LIFE AND THE DELTA-V BUDGET

Starlink is disposable; a data center is not, and 650 km will not clear in time. The 5-year delta-v budget is small in propellant but dominated by disposal:
   Δv_deorbit = v_c1 · (1 − √(2·r2 / (r1 + r2))) ≈ 132 m/s
out of ~190 m/s total (drag make-up ~13, formation ~8, avoidance ~5, de-orbit ~132, margin). By the rocket equation m_p = m_dry·(e^(Δv/(Isp·g0)) − 1), that is ~34 kg of hydrazine or ~5 kg with electric propulsion, which pairs naturally with the abundant solar power but is slow and must be coordinated through the formation.
(Figure 1 and Figure 5: decay and delta-v.)


10. COMPUTE AND WORKLOAD: USEFUL FLOPS, NOT PEAK

The point of the system is delivered, useful compute:
   delivered_PFLOPS = n_chips · peak · MFU · radiation_availability
Model-FLOPs utilization is ~40 to 60% for training but only ~8 to 10% for inference decode, so a space inference platform should be planned around the low regime. The honest efficiency metric is an effective PUE that folds in the radiation throughput tax, and the honest cost metric is dollars per useful PFLOP-hour (peak figures must be divided by MFU, raising effective cost 3 to 10x). Terrestrial best-in-class PUE is ~1.09 and the industry average ~1.56; space dodges cooling and water overhead but pays in mass, radiators, and launch, so the comparison must be made on total system cost, not PUE.
(Figure K: cost of useful compute; throughput tax.)


11. LAUNCH AND ECONOMICS

Per-satellite cost is launch plus hardware. With 415 kg and ~1.1 million USD of hardware, launch equals hardware at ~2,650 USD/kg; below ~600 USD/kg, launch is a minor term. So the economics are gated by hardware cost, lifetime, utilization, the radiation tax, and debris-driven replenishment, not by the headline dollar-per-kilogram. The launch-cost trajectory (Shuttle ~54,500, Falcon 9 ~3,770, Starship target 30 to 600 USD/kg) enabled the concept but is no longer the swing variable.
(Figure 6: cost vs launch price.)


12. THE INTEGRATED PICTURE

Closing all of this into one self-consistent design (power to array to mass to thermal to delta-v to economics) shows the message clearly on a likelihood-consequence matrix: cooling sits in the low-risk corner; debris cascade, end-of-life disposal, formation autonomy, and economics sit in the high-risk corner. Effort and demonstrator budget should follow the risk, not the physics that is easiest to model.
(Figure 7: systems risk matrix.)


CONCLUSIONS

1. Cooling is real and flight-proven, but it does not scale for free, and big chips need active cooling at the die.
2. Power is favorable in a dawn-dusk orbit, but overhead dominates small buses; efficiency needs scale.
3. The bus cannot point the laser; a fine-steering terminal is mandatory.
4. The tight formation is what makes terabit links work and what makes debris and avoidance dangerous.
5. Radiation is survivable but taxes throughput 10 to 20%.
6. No on-orbit repair makes availability a sparing and replenishment problem.
7. Debris in a tight formation is the binding, unsolved constraint.
8. Disposal requires propulsion; 650 km fails the 5-year rule naturally.
9. Useful compute, not peak, sets the real economics; launch cost is no longer decisive.

The constructive message: cooling already works. The demonstrators should target debris autonomy, optical-pointing in formation, and credible disposal, because those, not heat, decide whether constellation-scale orbital AI is viable. The right next mission is the small, attritable demonstrator the field is already flying.
