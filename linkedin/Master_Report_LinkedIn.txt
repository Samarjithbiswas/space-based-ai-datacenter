Space-based AI data centers stopped being a slide in 2025. Starcloud flew an NVIDIA H100 in orbit (Nov 2025). Axiom put data-center nodes in LEO (Jan 2026). Google's Project Suncatcher targets two TPU prototypes by early 2027.

So I did the full engineering study, from primary sources and open simulation, and I tried hard to separate what is demonstrated from what is marketing.

The honest verdict:

🛰️ Cooling is solved and flight-proven. But it does not scale: radiator area is linear in power (~1,500 m² per megawatt), and above ~400 W per chip the passive heat path alone blows the 125 C budget, so big GPUs need liquid cooling at the die.

☢️ Radiation is survivable, with margin. Google's TPU showed first memory irregularities at 2 krad and no hard failures to 15 krad, against a ~750 rad 5-year requirement. The real cost is a ~10-20% throughput tax from bit-flips.

☄️ Orbital debris in a tight formation is the binding, unsolved problem: ~17% chance of a catastrophic hit per 81-satellite cluster over 5 years, and the 150 m spacing that makes the terabit laser links work is exactly what makes a cascade and collision-avoidance dangerous.

🌍 It will not deorbit itself. At 650 km natural decay is ~22 years, failing the new FCC 5-year rule. Active de-orbit is mandatory.

💰 And the economics are later than advertised. The "$200/kg launch by 2035" that underwrites the whole case is fragile: my independent Monte-Carlo puts median cost-parity around 2044, not 2035.

Everything is open and reproducible: a 13-subsystem Python model, 80+ tests, full derivations, and the simulations behind every number. Repo in the comments.

The takeaway for the teams flying this: cooling already works. Aim the demonstrators at debris autonomy and credible disposal, because those, not heat, decide whether orbital AI scales.

SPACE-BASED AI DATA CENTERS: A COMPLETE ENGINEERING STUDY
Feasibility, Survivability, Mathematics, Simulations, and Predictions

By Samarjith Biswas, PhD  -  June 2026
Reference architecture: Google Project Suncatcher (arXiv:2511.19468). Every load-bearing fact is cited; every forward number is flagged as a projection; all code is open and reproducible.


WHAT IS ACTUALLY FLYING (verified, 2025-2026)

- Project Suncatcher (Google + Planet): an illustrative 81-satellite, 1 km-radius TPU constellation in a 650 km dawn-dusk sun-synchronous orbit; two prototypes targeted by early 2027.
- Starcloud-1: an NVIDIA H100 (80 GB, ~4 PFLOPS) on a ~60 kg satellite, launched 2 Nov 2025; vision of a 5 GW data center with ~4 km x 4 km arrays.
- Axiom Space: Orbital Data Center nodes reached LEO on 11 Jan 2026; stated kilowatt-to-megawatt scaling goal.

For honesty: Starcloud's "10x cheaper including launch" claim and an "Axiom nodes by end of 2025" claim both failed independent verification. Treat marketing economics with care. And the most load-bearing single source (the Suncatcher paper) is a non-peer-reviewed, vendor-authored preprint.


1. THERMAL: SOLVED, BUT IT DOES NOT SCALE

Radiated power per unit area is fixed by the fourth-power law, so radiator area is linear in heat load:

[IMAGE: eq_07_radiator_area.png]
   A_rad = Q / ( ε · σ · T⁴ · n_sides · (1 − f_p) )

A 1 MW node needs about 1,500 m² of double-sided radiator at 20 C, consistent with the ~1,200 m²/MW industry rule and with Starcloud's multi-kilometer panel vision for gigawatt scale. There is a second, sharper wall at the chip. With a passive thermal chain,

[IMAGE: eq_08_junction_temp.png]
   T_j = T_rad + Q · R_th

at R_th = 0.30 K/W the rise across the interface alone is 90 C at 300 W but 210 C at 700 W and 360 C at 1200 W, exceeding the entire 125 C budget. Above ~400 W per chip, liquid or loop-heat-pipe cooling at the die is mandatory, which is why Starcloud and Axiom use pumped loops.

[FIGURE: fig2_thermal_wall.png]


2. END OF LIFE: DRAG, DECAY, AND THE 5-YEAR RULE

Atmospheric drag slowly lowers the orbit. From the orbital-energy balance, the decay rate is:

[IMAGE: eq_05_orbital_decay.png]
   da/dt = − C_D · (A/m) · ρ(h) · √(μ a)

Integrating with a real atmosphere model, the reference satellite at 650 km decays in about 22 years (4 years at solar maximum, 312 years at solar minimum). The FCC now requires deorbit within 5 years (binding since 2022, effective 2024). So active de-orbit propulsion is mandatory, not optional.

[FIGURE: fig1_orbital_decay.png]


3. RADIATION IS SURVIVABLE, WITH MARGIN

Dose behind aluminium falls roughly exponentially atop a proton floor:

[IMAGE: eq_17_dose_depth.png]
   D(x) = D₀ · e^(−x/λ) + D_∞

Behind 10 mm of aluminium the 5-year dose is about 1 krad, against a ~750 rad requirement. Google's TPU showed first memory irregularities at 2 krad and no hard failures to 15 krad: comfortable margin. The real cost is single-event upsets; because AI inference tolerates bit-flips, the right design is error-correction plus checkpointing (about 86% usable compute), not full triple redundancy (which halves throughput). Carry the ~10-20% throughput tax explicitly.

[FIGURE: fig4_radiation.png]


4. ORBITAL DEBRIS AND THE FORMATION PARADOX

Catastrophic impacts are rare independent events (Poisson). The probability of at least one in the cluster is:

[IMAGE: eq_18_debris_risk.png]
   P = 1 − e^(−Φ A N t)

With a representative flux this is about 17% per 81-satellite cluster over 5 years (band 6-46%). Worse, the tight formation couples failures: one fragmentation sprays lethal fragments, and a neighbor at spacing d is hit with

[IMAGE: eq_19_cascade.png]
   P_hit = 1 − exp( − N_f · r_t² / (4 d²) )

about 8.5% per neighbor at 150 m. Because this scales as 1/d², widening to 1 km cuts it below 1% but breaks the laser-link budget. That is the formation paradox, and it is the binding, unsolved constraint.

[FIGURE: fig3_debris_risk.png]


5. WHY THE FORMATION IS TIGHT (THE LINK BUDGET)

Optical links are range-bound. Telescope gain and free-space loss are:

[IMAGE: eq_15_telescope_gain.png]   G = (π D / λ)²
[IMAGE: eq_16_free_space_loss.png]   L_fs = (4 π R / λ)²

With realized terminal parameters (1550 nm, 80 mm telescope, 15 microradian divergence, 1 microradian pointing, 10 Gbps), a 1 W link with 3 dB margin is reliable only to about 5,419 km, and path loss rises 20 dB per decade of range. Suncatcher needs roughly 10 Tbps per link, achievable with off-the-shelf wavelength multiplexing only by flying satellites about 150 m apart. The tight cluster is forced by the inverse-square link physics.


6. DE-ORBIT, POWER, POINTING, COMPUTE (in brief)

- De-orbit by a Hohmann burn:
[IMAGE: eq_20_deorbit_dv.png]   Δv = v_c1 · (1 − √( 2 r₂ / (r₁ + r₂) ) ) ≈ 132 m/s
  This dominates the ~190 m/s 5-year budget; propellant by the rocket equation is ~5 kg with electric propulsion.
- Power: a dawn-dusk orbit is >95% sunlit, so the battery is tiny; triple-junction arrays deliver ~253 W/m² at end of life.
- Pointing: a laser link needs sub-microradian accuracy; a star tracker gives ~10, so a fine-steering mirror is mandatory. The bus cannot point the beam.
- Compute: useful output is peak × utilization × radiation-availability. Inference utilization is only ~10%, so real cost per useful PFLOP-hour is several times the peak figure.

[FIGURE: fig5_deltav.png]


7. PREDICTION: COST PARITY IS THE SWING VARIABLE, AND IT IS FRAGILE

The entire economic case rests on launch reaching about $200/kg, at which point launched-power price approaches terrestrial data-center power spend. The vendor projects this by ~2035. I tested it with an independent Monte-Carlo (a learning curve on cumulative launched mass, sampling learning rate, payload, cadence, and starting price). The result: median parity around 2044, with a 5th percentile of 2037 and essentially no chance of 2035. The vendor date sits at the optimistic edge and needs its specific aggressive assumptions.

Prediction: cost parity is more likely late-2030s to mid-2040s than 2035, and it is necessary but not sufficient. Hardware cost, lifetime, and utilization gate the economics regardless.

[FIGURE: fig_cost_parity.png]


8. THE HONEST PICTURE

Demonstrated today: one H100 in orbit, ground TPU radiation testing, a 1.6 Tbps bench link, kilowatt-class nodes. Required for the vision: megawatt-to-gigawatt clusters, flown 10 Tbps formation links, $200/kg launch, and in-flight formation station-keeping. None of those is demonstrated. The binding milestones are not thermal; they are autonomous debris avoidance at tight spacing, credible propulsive disposal, a flown megawatt-class radiator, and launch economics actually reaching the parity band.


CONCLUSION

Cooling already works. Radiation is survivable. The real frontier is debris survivability in a tight formation, credible disposal, and economics that are later and less certain than advertised. The right program is what is already flying: small, attritable demonstrators that retire the survivability and autonomy risks. Build those, measure in flight, and gate constellation-scale deployment behind a demonstrated answer to the formation/debris paradox, not behind a launch-cost milestone.
