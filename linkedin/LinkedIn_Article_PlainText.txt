Space-based AI data centers stopped being a slide in 2025. Starcloud flew an NVIDIA H100 in orbit (Nov 2025). Axiom put data-center nodes in LEO (Jan 2026). Google's Project Suncatcher targets two TPU prototypes by early 2027.

So I did the full engineering study, from primary sources and open simulation, and tried hard to separate what is demonstrated from what is marketing.

The verdict:

Cooling is solved and flight-proven, but it does not scale gracefully. Radiator size grows in direct proportion to power, on the order of 1,500 square meters per megawatt, and once a single chip pushes past about 400 watts the passive heat path alone breaks the 125 C budget. Big accelerators need liquid cooling right at the die.

Radiation is survivable, with margin. Google's TPU showed first memory irregularities around 2 krad and no hard failures out to 15 krad, against a 5-year requirement near 750 rad. The real cost is a 10 to 20 percent throughput tax from bit-flips, not a wall.

Orbital debris in a tight formation is the binding, unsolved problem. There is roughly a 17 percent chance of a catastrophic hit per 81-satellite cluster over five years, and the close spacing that makes the terabit laser links work is exactly what makes a collision cascade dangerous.

It will not deorbit itself. At 650 km the orbit takes about 22 years to decay naturally, which fails the new 5-year disposal rule. Active de-orbit is mandatory.

And the economics are later than advertised. The whole case rests on launch reaching about 200 dollars per kilogram. My independent Monte-Carlo says that price is not even reached in most plausible scenarios within the horizon, and where it is reached the median is the mid-2040s, not 2035.

Everything is open: a Python model, a full test suite, all derivations, and the simulations behind every number. Repo in the comments.

The takeaway for the teams flying this: cooling already works. Aim the demonstrators at debris autonomy and credible disposal, because those, not heat, decide whether orbital AI scales.

SPACE-BASED AI DATA CENTERS: A COMPLETE ENGINEERING STUDY
Feasibility, survivability, and economics, from primary sources

By Samarjith Biswas, PhD

Reference architecture: Google Project Suncatcher (arXiv:2511.19468). Every load-bearing fact is cited, every forward-looking number is flagged as a projection, and all the code is open.


WHAT IS ACTUALLY FLYING (2025 TO 2026)

Project Suncatcher (Google and Planet) is an illustrative 81-satellite TPU constellation in a 650 km dawn-dusk sun-synchronous orbit, with two prototypes targeted by early 2027.

Starcloud-1 carried an NVIDIA H100 on a roughly 60 kg satellite, launched 2 November 2025, with a stated long-term vision of a multi-gigawatt data center on kilometer-scale arrays.

Axiom Space reported Orbital Data Center nodes reaching low Earth orbit on 11 January 2026, with a kilowatt-to-megawatt scaling goal.

A caution on claims. Starcloud's "ten times cheaper including launch" line and an "Axiom nodes by end of 2025" line both failed independent verification. The single most load-bearing source, the Suncatcher paper itself, is a non-peer-reviewed, vendor-authored preprint. Treat the marketing economics with care.


1. THERMAL: SOLVED, BUT IT DOES NOT SCALE

A radiator rejects heat by infrared emission, and the power it can emit per unit area is fixed by temperature through the fourth-power radiation law. Because the emitted power per square meter is essentially constant for a given radiator temperature, the area you need grows in direct proportion to the heat you must dump.

A one-megawatt node needs roughly 1,500 square meters of double-sided radiator at about 20 C, which is consistent with the industry rule of around 1,200 square meters per megawatt and with Starcloud's kilometer-scale panel vision for gigawatt operation.

There is a second, sharper wall at the chip itself. Heat has to cross the thermal resistance between the die and the radiator, and the temperature rise across that path is simply the heat flow multiplied by that resistance. With a passive chain, the rise across the interface alone is about 90 C at 300 watts, but about 210 C at 700 watts and about 360 C at 1,200 watts, which already exceeds the entire 125 C budget on its own. Above roughly 400 watts per chip, liquid or loop-heat-pipe cooling at the die is mandatory, which is exactly why Starcloud and Axiom use pumped loops.

2. END OF LIFE: DRAG, DECAY, AND THE 5-YEAR RULE

Even at 650 km a thin atmosphere remains, and the drag it exerts slowly removes orbital energy, which lowers the orbit. The decay accelerates as the satellite drops into denser air.

Integrating that decay with a realistic atmosphere model, the reference satellite at 650 km takes about 22 years to come down under moderate solar activity, ranging from about 4 years at solar maximum to over 300 years at solar minimum. The disposal rule now requires reentry within 5 years. Passive decay therefore fails the rule, and active de-orbit propulsion is mandatory rather than optional.

[FIGURE: fig1_orbital_decay.png]


3. RADIATION IS SURVIVABLE, WITH MARGIN

Shielding cuts the radiation dose roughly exponentially with thickness, on top of a hard-to-stop proton floor. Behind about 10 mm of aluminium the five-year dose is on the order of 1 krad, against a requirement near 750 rad.

Google's TPU showed first memory irregularities around 2 krad and no hard failures out to 15 krad, which is comfortable margin. The real cost is single-event upsets, the bit-flips caused by individual particles. Because AI inference tolerates occasional bit-flips, the right design is error correction plus checkpointing, which keeps about 86 percent of the compute usable, rather than full triple redundancy, which would halve throughput. Carry that 10 to 20 percent throughput tax explicitly.

[FIGURE: fig4_radiation.png]


4. ORBITAL DEBRIS AND THE FORMATION PARADOX

Catastrophic impacts are rare, roughly independent events, so they follow Poisson statistics. With a representative debris flux, the chance of at least one catastrophic hit somewhere in the cluster is about 17 percent per 81-satellite cluster over five years, with a plausible band of roughly 6 to 46 percent.

Worse, a tight formation couples failures. One fragmentation sprays lethal fragments, and the chance that a given neighbor is hit rises sharply as the spacing shrinks, because the exposure scales with the inverse square of the distance. At 150 m spacing a single fragmentation puts roughly an 8.5 percent hit probability on each near neighbor. Widening the formation to a kilometer cuts that well below one percent, but it also breaks the laser-link budget. That tension is the formation paradox, and it is the binding, unsolved constraint.

[FIGURE: fig3_debris_risk.png]


5. WHY THE FORMATION IS TIGHT (THE LINK BUDGET)

Optical links are range-bound. A larger telescope concentrates the beam and raises gain, but free-space loss climbs steeply with distance. With realistic terminal parameters (1550 nm wavelength, an 80 mm telescope, tight beam divergence and pointing, 10 Gbps), a one-watt link with 3 dB of margin is reliable only out to about 5,400 km, and the loss rises about 20 dB for every tenfold increase in range.

Suncatcher needs on the order of 10 Tbps per link. With off-the-shelf wavelength multiplexing, that is achievable only by flying satellites roughly 150 m apart. The tight cluster is forced by the inverse-square physics of the link, not chosen for convenience.


6. DE-ORBIT, POWER, POINTING, AND COMPUTE (IN BRIEF)

De-orbit. Lowering the orbit for disposal takes a velocity change of about 132 m/s, which dominates the roughly 190 m/s five-year budget. With electric propulsion the propellant is only a few kilograms.

Power. A dawn-dusk orbit is sunlit more than 95 percent of the time, so the battery is small, and triple-junction solar arrays deliver on the order of 250 watts per square meter at end of life.

Pointing. A laser link needs sub-microradian accuracy, while a star tracker gives only about ten microradians, so a fine-steering mirror is mandatory. The spacecraft body cannot aim the beam by itself.

Compute. Useful output is peak performance multiplied by utilization and by radiation availability. Inference utilization is often only about ten percent, so the real cost per useful unit of compute is several times the headline peak figure.

7. PREDICTION: COST PARITY IS THE SWING VARIABLE, AND IT IS FRAGILE

The entire economic case rests on launch falling to about 200 dollars per kilogram, at which point the annualized cost of power in orbit approaches what terrestrial data centers spend on power. The vendor projects that price by about 2035.

I tested it with an independent Monte-Carlo, applying a learning curve to cumulative launched mass and sampling the uncertain inputs: the learning rate, the starting price, the payload, and the launch cadence. The first result is the one that matters most and is easy to miss in a single date. Across the sampled assumptions, only about five percent of scenarios reach 200 dollars per kilogram at all within the horizon to 2050. In other words, under most defensible assumptions that price is not reached. Among the minority of scenarios that do reach it, the median year is about 2044, with essentially no chance of 2035. Extending the horizon to 2060 lifts the reaching fraction only to about nine percent.

The prediction is therefore twofold. Parity at 200 dollars per kilogram is unlikely under most assumptions rather than merely late, and where it does arrive it is more likely in the late 2030s to mid-2040s than in 2035. Parity is in any case necessary but not sufficient, because even at parity the business case is gated by hardware cost, satellite lifetime, and utilization.

[FIGURE: fig_cost_parity.png]


8. THE HONEST PICTURE

Demonstrated today: one H100 in orbit, ground radiation testing of a TPU, a bench-scale terabit link, and kilowatt-class nodes. Required for the vision: megawatt-to-gigawatt clusters, flown multi-terabit formation links, far cheaper launch, and in-flight formation station-keeping. None of those is demonstrated yet. The binding milestones are not thermal. They are autonomous debris avoidance at tight spacing, credible propulsive disposal, a flown megawatt-class radiator, and launch economics that actually reach the parity band.


CONCLUSION

Cooling already works. Radiation is survivable. The real frontier is debris survivability in a tight formation, credible disposal, and economics that are later and less certain than advertised. The right program is the one already flying: small, attritable demonstrators that retire the survivability and autonomy risks. Build those, measure in flight, and gate constellation-scale deployment behind a demonstrated answer to the formation and debris paradox, not behind a launch-cost milestone alone.
