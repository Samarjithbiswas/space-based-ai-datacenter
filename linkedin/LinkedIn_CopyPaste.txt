I wrote that space-based AI data centers can stay cool. Two commenters pushed back: cooling is the easy part. They were right.

So I rebuilt the entire analysis from primary sources (NASA, ESA, FCC, the NRLMSIS atmosphere model, vendor datasheets, and Google's Project Suncatcher paper) and corrected my own mistakes in the process.

The honest verdict, with every number sourced:

🛰️ COOLING IS SOLVED. Starcloud flew an Nvidia H100 in orbit in November 2025 and trained a model on it. The physics works.

🔥 BUT IT DOES NOT SCALE FOR FREE. Radiator area grows linearly with power (about 1,500 m² per megawatt). And above roughly 400 W per chip, the passive heat path alone exceeds the entire 125 °C budget, so H100 and Blackwell-class parts need liquid cooling right at the die.

☄️ DEBRIS IS THE REAL FRONTIER. An 81-satellite cluster faces a central ~17% chance of a catastrophic impact larger than 1 cm over 5 years. At sub-200 m spacing, one fragmentation has about an 8.5% chance of striking each neighbor: the seed of a cascade.

🌍 IT WILL NOT DEORBIT ITSELF. At 650 km the natural decay time is about 22 years, which fails the new FCC 5-year rule. Active de-orbit is mandatory (about 132 m/s, the largest item in the whole delta-v budget).

💰 LAUNCH COST IS NO LONGER DECISIVE. Below about 600 USD/kg, hardware dominates the cost, not launch.

The message for the teams actually flying this hardware: cooling already works. The demonstrators should target debris autonomy and credible disposal, because those, not heat, decide whether orbital AI scales.

Full article with the complete mathematical derivations and all figures is linked below. Every model is open and reproducible. Corrections welcome.

SPACE-BASED AI DATA CENTERS, PART II
Survivability, Reliability, and Launch Architecture: A Verified Systems Assessment with Full Mathematical Foundation

By Samarjith Biswas, PhD
An independent engineering assessment, continuation of a November 2025 thermal study. Reference system: Google Project Suncatcher (arXiv:2511.19468).


ABSTRACT

A prior study showed that passive radiative cooling can keep a TPU-class accelerator within its temperature limit in low Earth orbit. This continuation tests the harder question that study excluded: whether the system survives a multi-year mission in the real LEO environment, and whether it can be launched and disposed of responsibly. Every input has been re-verified against primary sources, and several figures from the first study are corrected. The headline result is that thermal management, now flight-demonstrated by Starcloud's orbital H100, is the solved part of the problem. The unsolved parts are orbital-debris survivability in a tight formation, mandatory active de-orbit, and economics that do not yet close.


1. WHY A PART II

This is an honest revision, not a defense of prior work. Two readers were right to push on the boundary the first study drew. One asked for a reproducible, harmonized environment model. The other argued that the hardest part of space is not steady-state heat balance but survivability: radiation, debris, collision avoidance, and disposal. A third reason is internal: the first study contained one outright error and one optimistic assumption. Both are corrected below.


2. WHAT I CORRECTED (being honest is the point)

• Disposal rule: the LEO rule is now 5 years, not 25 (FCC, since 2022, effective 2024). At 650 km the natural lifetime is about 22 years, so it fails the rule. Active de-orbit is mandatory.
• Chip power: Google publishes no TDP for the TPU v6e; the best estimate is about 150 to 200 W, not the 300 W I assumed.
• Atmospheric density at 650 km: 1.6 x 10^-13 kg/m³ (moderate solar), swinging about 100x over the solar cycle. My earlier value was about 2.5x too low.
• Debris flux above 1 cm: 1 x 10^-5 to 1 x 10^-4 per m² per year (corrected upward).
• Radiator accounting: double-sided is physical; corrected.
• Coating ageing: the dominant mode is solar absorptance rising from 0.15 to about 0.36; emissivity stays near 0.90.


3. THE VERIFIED REFERENCE SYSTEM (confirmed from the Suncatcher paper)

• Orbit: dawn-dusk sun-synchronous, mean altitude 650 km.
• Cluster: 81 satellites on a 1 km-radius lattice, with 100 to 200 m spacing to close the optical links.
• Optical link: 800 Gbps one-way (1.6 Tbps two-way) at 1.55 micrometres.
• Accelerator: TPU v6e Trillium, 918 TFLOPS BF16, 32 GB HBM. Radiation-tested in a 67 MeV proton beam at UC Davis; 5-year requirement 750 rad(Si); HBM is the limiter at about 2 krad(Si).
• Chips actually flying: Starcloud flew one Nvidia H100 (700 W) in orbit in Nov 2025; Starcloud-2 (Oct 2026) brings B200 (1000 W) and GB200-class parts (1200 W per GPU). Per-chip power is climbing fast.


4. ORBITAL-MECHANICS PRELIMINARIES

For a circular orbit, gravity equals the centripetal force, so:
   v = √(μ / a)          T = 2π · √(a³ / μ)
At 650 km (a = 7.021 x 10^6 m): v = 7,534.8 m/s and T = 97.6 min, which reproduces the Suncatcher orbital period. Specific orbital energy is E = -μ / (2a), used in the decay derivation.


5. END OF LIFE: ATMOSPHERIC DRAG AND ORBITAL DECAY

Finding. A data center is not disposable like a Starlink unit, and at 650 km it will not decay in time. Natural lifetime is about 22 years (moderate solar), about 4 years at solar maximum, and about 312 years at solar minimum. Only a solar-maximum launch comes close to the 5-year rule. Active de-orbit is mandatory.

Drag deceleration (per unit mass):
   a_D = (1/2) · ρ · v² · C_D · (A/m)

Derivation. Drag removes orbital energy at the rate dE/dt = -a_D · v. For a near-circular orbit E = -μ/(2a), so dE/dt = (μ / 2a²) · da/dt. Equating the two and using v = √(μ/a) gives the decay rate:

   da/dt = - C_D · (A/m) · ρ(h) · √(μ · a)

Because density rises steeply as the orbit lowers, decay accelerates into a rapid re-entry. The equation is integrated numerically to re-entry at 120 km using NRLMSIS density, with the 100x solar-cycle swing shown as an uncertainty band.

(Figure 1: natural lifetime vs altitude; at 650 km it fails the 5-year rule.)


6. THERMAL: FEASIBILITY CONFIRMED, BUT IT DOES NOT SCALE

Finding. Cooling is flight-proven, but it does not compound, and a second wall appears at the chip interface.

From Planck's law, integrating over a hemisphere and all wavelengths gives the Stefan-Boltzmann result E = σ·T⁴. A gray panel radiating to the 2.725 K microwave background rejects:
   Q_rad ≈ ε · σ · A · T⁴

Required radiator area for a load Q at temperature T (double-sided, parasitic fraction f_p):
   A_rad = Q / [ ε · σ · T⁴ · n_sides · (1 - f_p) ]

This is LINEAR in Q: no economy of scale. Worked example (1 MW, 20 °C, ε = 0.90): ε·σ·T⁴ = 376.9 W/m² per side; net = 376.9 x 2 x 0.88 = 663.3 W/m²; A_rad = 1,000,000 / 663.3 = 1,508 m². This matches the industry "about 1,200 m² per MW" rule. At 60 °C the area falls to about 900 m².

The interface wall. Heat flows junction to radiator through series resistances:
   T_j = T_rad + Q · R_th
With the first study's passive chain R_th = 0.30 K/W, the rise across the interface alone is P x 0.30: that is 90 °C at 300 W, but 210 °C at 700 W and 360 °C at 1200 W. Since the entire junction budget is 125 °C, any chip above about 417 W cannot be cooled passively at any radiator size. H100-class and larger parts need liquid or loop-heat-pipe cooling at the die.

(Figure 2: radiator area vs power; interface temperature rise vs chip.)


7. RADIATION AND RELIABILITY: A TAX, NOT A WALL

Finding. Total ionizing dose is survivable with shielding; single-event upsets impose a throughput tax of order 10 to 20%.

Dose behind aluminium of thickness x:
   D(x) = D0 · e^(-x / λ) + D_inf,   with λ ≈ 1.7 mm
calibrated to flight data (SIRI-1 at 600 km SSO and the Suncatcher proton test), giving a deep-shield floor of about 100 to 150 rad/yr. Worked example (10 mm, 5 years): about 1.06 krad(Si), under the 2 krad HBM tolerance with about 1.9x margin; meeting Google's 750 rad requirement needs about 12 mm.

Because AI inference tolerates bit-flips far better than traditional HPC, the right design is ECC plus checkpointing (about 86% usable compute), not full triple-modular redundancy (which halves throughput).

(Figure 4: dose vs shielding; reliability vs throughput trade.)


8. ORBITAL DEBRIS AND THE IN-CLUSTER CASCADE: THE BINDING CONSTRAINT

Finding. Debris survivability in a tight formation is the binding constraint, more than any thermal or radiation question.

Catastrophic-impact probability (Poisson model): for flux Φ, cross-section A, N satellites, time t:
   P(at least one hit) = 1 - e^(-Φ · A · N · t)
Worked example (Φ = 3 x 10^-5 per m² per year, A = 15 m², N = 81, t = 5): probability = 1 - e^(-0.182) = 16.7%. The flux band maps to 6% to 46% over 5 years, and about 31% over 10 years.

Cascade coupling. A break-up sends N_f lethal fragments over a sphere of area 4π·d² at range d. A neighbor of cross-section π·r_t² is struck with probability:
   P_hit(d) = 1 - exp( - N_f · r_t² / (4 · d²) )
Worked example (N_f = 2000, r_t = 2 m, d = 150 m): P_hit = 8.5% per neighbor. Since P_hit scales as 1/d², widening the formation to 1 km drops it below 1%, but that degrades the optical-link budget. This is the formation paradox. Ground tracking also cannot see the sub-10 cm fragments that dominate the catastrophic flux.

(Figure 3: catastrophic risk vs time; cascade coupling vs spacing.)


9. STATION-KEEPING AND THE FORMATION PARADOX

Holding a sub-200 m formation at 650 km is hard because drag is small but varies about 100x over the solar cycle and acts differently on each satellite. The propellant cost is small; the difficulty is control and sensing. The paradox: the same tight spacing that enables terabit optical links makes collision avoidance dangerous, because a satellite dodging debris becomes a hazard to its neighbors.


10. THE DELTA-V BUDGET AND DISPOSAL PROPULSION

Finding. The 5-year budget is small in propellant (about 190 m/s) but dominated by the about 132 m/s de-orbit term, the line item that separates a disposable satellite from a permanent one.

Drag make-up over the mission: about 13 m/s.
Controlled de-orbit (single retrograde burn lowering perigee from r1 to r2 = 180 km), from the vis-viva equation:
   Δv_deorbit = v_c1 · ( 1 - √( 2·r2 / (r1 + r2) ) )
Worked example (r1 = 7.021 x 10^6 m, r2 = 6.551 x 10^6 m, v_c1 = 7,534.8 m/s): Δv = 131.6 m/s.

Propellant (rocket equation), for dry mass m_dry:
   m_p = m_dry · ( e^( Δv / (I_sp · g0) ) - 1 )
Worked example (Δv = 189.5 m/s, m_dry = 375 kg): 34.4 kg with hydrazine (I_sp = 220 s) or only 4.9 kg with electric propulsion (I_sp = 1500 s). Electric is mass-efficient and pairs with the abundant solar power, but is slow and must be coordinated through the formation.

A dawn-dusk sun-synchronous orbit holds the beta angle near the critical value arcsin(R_E / (R_E + h)) = 65.1 degrees at 650 km, giving a greater than 95% illumination duty cycle and minimal thermal cycling.

(Figure 5: delta-v budget; propellant by propulsion type.)


11. LAUNCH ARCHITECTURE AND ECONOMICS

Finding. Launch cost is no longer the binding constraint. Per-satellite cost is launch plus hardware. With 415 kg launch mass and about 1.1 million USD of hardware, launch equals hardware at about 2,650 USD/kg; below about 600 USD/kg launch is a small correction. The economics are gated by hardware cost, lifetime, utilization, the radiation throughput tax, and debris-driven replenishment, not by the headline dollar-per-kilogram. The launch-cost trajectory (Shuttle about 54,500 USD/kg, Falcon 9 about 3,770 USD/kg, Starship target band 30 to 600 USD/kg) is necessary but no longer sufficient.

(Figure 6: per-satellite cost vs launch price; the launch-cost trajectory.)


12. WHERE THE REAL RISK LIVES

Placing every finding on a likelihood-consequence matrix makes the message unambiguous: cooling sits in the low-risk corner; the debris cascade, end-of-life disposal, and economics sit in the high-risk corner. Effort and demonstrator budget should follow the risk, not the physics that is easiest to model.

(Figure 7: systems-level risk matrix.)


13. RECOMMENDED DEMONSTRATOR

Precisely what the field is already doing. A small, attritable, one- or two-satellite demonstrator that:
1. measures in-situ radiation dose and upset rates on the target chip;
2. demonstrates a modular liquid or loop-cooled radiator at real kilowatt-class chip power;
3. demonstrates onboard debris sensing and an autonomous avoidance maneuver;
4. demonstrates a controlled, propulsive de-orbit at end of mission.
Constellation-scale deployment should be gated behind a demonstrated answer to the debris and formation problem, not behind a launch-cost milestone.


14. CONCLUSIONS

1. Thermal feasibility is real and flight-proven at the single-node scale.
2. Thermal does not scale for free; the passive interface saturates above about 400 W, forcing chip-level active cooling for the GPUs being deployed.
3. Radiation is survivable but imposes a 10 to 20% throughput tax.
4. Debris in a tight formation is the binding, unsolved constraint (about 17% central 5-year catastrophic probability per cluster).
5. End of life requires propulsion; 650 km fails the 5-year disposal rule naturally.
6. Launch cost is no longer decisive; hardware, lifetime, and utilization govern.

Cooling is the part that already works. The demonstrators should target debris autonomy and credible disposal, because those, not heat, decide whether constellation-scale orbital AI is viable.


SUMMARY OF GOVERNING EQUATIONS

• Orbital decay:   da/dt = - C_D · (A/m) · ρ · √(μ·a)
• Radiator area:   A_rad = Q / [ ε · σ · T⁴ · n · (1 - f_p) ]
• Junction temp:   T_j = T_rad + Q · R_th
• Dose vs depth:   D(x) = D0 · e^(-x/λ) + D_inf
• Debris risk:     P = 1 - e^(-Φ·A·N·t)
• Cascade:         P_hit = 1 - exp(-N_f·r_t² / (4·d²))
• De-orbit Δv:     Δv = v_c1 · (1 - √(2·r2 / (r1 + r2)))
• Propellant:      m_p = m_dry · (e^(Δv/(I_sp·g0)) - 1)
